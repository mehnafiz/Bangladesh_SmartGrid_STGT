## 9.1 Principal Findings

### 9.1.1 Overall contribution

The completed study delivers an empirically frozen Correlation-Aware Multi-Task Forecasting Framework that forecasts next-day regional evening-peak demand and a graph-level Operational Stress Index from a single spatio-temporal graph-transformer model \cite{Vaswani2017Attention,Kipf2017GCN} on the Bangladesh national power system \cite{Hossain2025BangladeshSmartGrid}. The principal finding is not superiority on every isolated metric, but the demonstration that one architecture can supply operationally coupled day-ahead load and stress visibility from shared representations without reverting to separate single-task pipelines.

That joint capability matters because regional dispatch and system-pressure monitoring are ordinarily served by different models or post-hoc indicators. The frozen evaluation programme—spanning external benchmarks, configuration ablations, architecture selection, error characterisation, and post-hoc attribution—shows that the final correlation-graph PF-STGT configuration (S2) was selected through evidence rather than design intent alone. Scientifically, the work positions graph-transformer multi-task learning as a viable modelling paradigm for developing-economy grids where limitation dynamics and inter-regional load coupling coexist in daily operational records. Practically, operators gain a single checkpoint that supports paired load-and-stress dashboards instead of reconciling disconnected forecasts after the fact.

### 9.1.2 Interaction between demand forecasting and stress prediction

A central finding is that demand and OSI forecasting are complementary tasks within one model rather than redundant projections of the same signal. Stress-capable baselines in the benchmark suite show that classical ensembles can attach auxiliary stress outputs, yet their stress explanations of variance remain materially weaker than those of the proposed multi-task graph-transformer, while shallow recurrent and graph-convolution architectures fail to produce informative OSI forecasts at all. Within the configuration suite, lower demand error does not automatically imply better stress fidelity: geographically specialised and demand-only variants illustrate a genuine demand–stress trade-off space rather than a single Pareto-optimal point.

The interpretation is that shared encoding captures calendar, limitation, and grid-context structure relevant to both targets, while task-specific heads preserve distinct output semantics. Multi-task training \cite{Caruana1997Multitask,Song2024MultiTaskEnergy} under the repaired W20 protocol restores stress learnability that earlier configurations lacked, indicating that task balancing—not architecture alone—gates joint success. Scientifically, the study supports multi-task formulation as a principled response to coupled operational questions rather than as an incidental add-on to demand modelling. For day-ahead operations, the practical value lies in synchronised update cycles: the same input window yields regional megawatt trajectories and a national stress scalar suitable for joint review in control-room workflows.

### 9.1.3 Architecture selection and empirical simplification

Another principal finding is that evidence-driven simplification of the graph prior improves the frozen model relative to the historical hybrid geographical–correlation design. Correlation-only adjacency emerges as the strongest graph variant among those evaluated for joint demand and stress performance, whereas geographical-only conditioning degrades demand accuracy and removing the graph module on the hybrid trunk does not yield a statistically distinguishable gain. The architecture-selection record further shows that simplification is not uniformly beneficial: eliminating the temporal transformer on the correlation graph results in a marked degradation of demand performance, even though transformer removal on the hybrid reference is statistically indistinguishable on demand error.

The interpretation is that predictive inter-region coupling in this dataset is better encoded by train-estimated correlation structure than by static geographical proximity alone, and that the temporal branch appears to play a critical role once the graph prior is correlation-based. Scientifically, the result cautions against importing composite graph designs from other domains without ablative justification on the target system. Practically, deployment should adopt the correlation-graph, full-transformer, multi-task configuration frozen as S2 rather than lighter variants that appear attractive on isolated ablation rows.

### 9.1.4 Regional robustness and error structure

Regional analysis reveals a robust but heterogeneous forecaster: peripheral divisions achieve comparatively low absolute error, while the national load centre dominates the megawatt error budget across model families. The proposed model improves the capital division relative to earlier PF-STGT and strong classical references without eliminating the structural concentration of residual mass in the largest division. Macro-averaged scores therefore understate planning significance for the principal load hub even when they support a favourable national summary.

The interpretation is that regional robustness in this study means stable multi-division service with predictable error geography, not uniform accuracy everywhere. Error concentration in the highest-variability division persists across architectures, suggesting that remaining demand difficulty is tied to scale and volatility of the dominant node rather than to a failure of graph conditioning alone. Scientifically, the finding reinforces the need to report division-level diagnostics alongside macro metrics in multi-node national forecasts. Practically, operators should treat Dhaka as a separately reported diagnostic in deployment monitoring while relying on the model for consistent peripheral and mid-scale division service.

### 9.1.5 Explainability contribution

Post-hoc attribution on the frozen S2 checkpoint shows that stress and demand predictions draw on interpretable coalition families—limitation and calendar structure for OSI, calendar and autoregressive lag channels for the principal load division—while spatial mass concentrates on high-load nodes with moderate alignment between learned attention and the correlation adjacency. Dual-path comparison between gradient attributions and OSI component drivers achieves partial, not universal, agreement across stratified case studies, and demand attributions disagree with permutation-based importance rankings.

The interpretation is that explainability artefacts offer complementary transparency into model behaviour rather than independent validation of physical mechanisms. Coherent global and spatial attributions support operator trust in the sense of traceable inputs, but partial cross-method and dual-path disagreement forbids treating attribution plots as ground-truth causal maps. Scientifically, the study demonstrates multi-method post-hoc analysis in a national-scale graph-transformer forecasting setting, with explicit reporting of discordance where methods diverge. Practically, limitation-stack and calendar coalitions should headline stress explanation dashboards, while demand explanations should present gradient-based and permutation-based views separately rather than collapsing them into a single ranked list.

### 9.1.6 Scientific significance

Taken together, the principal findings establish that correlation-graph PF-STGT multi-task learning can jointly model regional demand and operational stress on a chronologically held-out year of Bangladesh grid data with statistically supported improvement over the historical hybrid reference on the primary demand objective and markedly stronger stress explanatory power within the frozen benchmark suite. The study contributes an evidence-locked modelling chain—from task repair and benchmark comparison through ablation, architecture freeze, error geography, and attribution—that is reproducible without rerunning training.

Scientifically, the work extends spatio-temporal deep forecasting beyond load-only benchmarks to coupled stress indices derived from operational limitation dynamics, and it shows that graph priors and multi-task objectives must be empirically tuned rather than assumed from domain intuition. For power-system practice in data-rich developing economies, the implication is that day-ahead joint load-and-stress forecasting is feasible as a single-model service when graph structure, task weighting, and division-level monitoring are co-designed with the evaluation evidence reported in Section 8.

## 9.2 Comparison with Existing Literature

### 9.2.1 Graph-based spatial forecasting

The present study finds that train-estimated correlation adjacency outperforms geographical-only and hybrid geographical–correlation priors for joint regional demand and OSI forecasting on the Bangladesh national grid, while a shallow spatio-temporal graph-convolution baseline demonstrates substantially lower predictive performance than the graph-transformer family in the frozen benchmark suite. Prior graph-neural work in power systems—represented in the reviewed corpus by a small set of high-relevance spatio-temporal GNN and graph-attention studies \cite{Shi2024EVChargingGNN,Zhuang2024MultiScaleGNN,Wu2023TransferLearningGNN}—typically couples nodes through geographic proximity, charging-network topology, or integrated-energy-system structure on US and European case studies rather than on developing-economy division-level operational records.

That contrast aligns with a recurring limitation in the literature: graph designs validated on residential, microgrid, or AMI-centric networks do not transfer automatically to a nine-division national system where inter-region demand coupling is strong and limitation dynamics are nationally co-visible. Recent spatio-temporal graph-attention and multi-graph convolution studies \cite{Zhao2024GraphAttentionTransformer,Bentsen2023GraphTransformer,Sun2024GLFN} demonstrate strong performance within their native topologies but seldom co-design graph priors with limitation-stack covariates or national stress targets. The scientific implication is that data-driven correlation graphs can encode predictive spatial structure where static geography alone is insufficient, extending the graph-forecasting line beyond topology imported from infrastructure maps. A remaining gap is the absence of external replication on other South Asian or similarly structured national grids; the correlation-graph advantage documented here is evidence-backed for Bangladesh but not established as a universal graph-construction rule.

### 9.2.2 Transformer-based temporal modelling

The frozen architecture-selection record shows that temporal transformer capacity appears critical when the graph prior is correlation-based, whereas transformer removal on the historical hybrid reference is not statistically distinguishable on demand error—a pattern not commonly reported in transformer forecasting papers that evaluate a single backbone on a fixed graph. Transformer-based load forecasting \cite{Wu2021Autoformer,Helmy2024AutoformerEV} constitutes a larger but still minority share of the reviewed literature, often applied to residential sequences, EV charging, or multivariate energy series without multi-node national stress heads.

Compared with those studies, the present work does not claim a novel temporal operator; rather, it shows that transformer contribution is conditional on graph topology in a multi-task national setting. Near-uniform temporal attention weights further suggest limited lag selectivity, which diverges from narratives that attribute transformer gains primarily to sharp recency focus. Scientifically, the finding cautions that transformer value in spatio-temporal energy forecasting should be reported together with graph context and ablation evidence, not assumed from single-configuration benchmarks. The remaining gap is whether alternative temporal encoders could match transformer performance on correlation graphs with lower complexity—an question the present simplification study begins but does not exhaust.

### 9.2.3 Multi-task demand and operational stress learning

A principal outcome of this study is that regional demand and graph-level OSI can be learned jointly from shared representations when task weighting is explicitly repaired, yielding a favourable joint configuration relative to single-task and stress-weak baselines, while still exhibiting a documented demand–stress trade-off against a demand-only upper bound. The reviewed literature rarely couples continuous multi-node demand forecasting with operational-stress-style targets in one model: multi-task entries in the corpus are sparse, and the shedding cluster predominantly addresses control, optimisation, or under-frequency response rather than day-ahead forecasting of coupled load and pressure indices.

Relative to hierarchical multi-energy multi-task proposals \cite{Song2024MultiTaskEnergy,Guillen2026LoadComposition}, the present formulation shifts emphasis from sectoral energy vectors to limitation-aware operational stress alongside evening-peak demand—closer to operational-stress gaps in the literature than to classical load-only multi-task extensions. Classical ensembles in the frozen benchmark can emit auxiliary stress outputs, which superficially resembles multi-target learning, but the literature seldom evaluates whether such add-on heads share representations meaningfully with spatial graph encoders. The scientific implication is that multi-task learning in energy systems is shown to be viable within the evaluated Bangladesh forecasting setting when tasks are operationally related yet statistically non-redundant, but not when treated as a default regulariser without loss calibration. The remaining gap is limited cross-dataset evidence on task-interference regimes: the W20 repair and ablation trade-offs are documented for one national timeline, not for a family of stress definitions or shedding formulations.

### 9.2.4 Explainability in graph-temporal forecasting

Post-hoc attribution on the frozen model shows coherent coalition-level and spatial patterns—limitation and calendar structure for stress, calendar and lag channels for the principal load division—with partial dual-path agreement against OSI component drivers and discordance between gradient-based and permutation-based demand rankings. Explainability appears in only a small fraction of the reviewed corpus; where present, it often serves microgrid shedding control, distillation, or generic interpretable ML frameworks rather than multi-node graph-transformer forecasts with operator-facing dual-task outputs.

Compared with SHAP-centric shedding studies \cite{Wang2026SHAPLoadShedding,Lundberg2017SHAP} and review papers that catalogue XAI methods \cite{Baur2024XAIReview} for scalar load series, the present work embeds explainability inside a national multi-task graph-transformer pipeline and reports method disagreement explicitly rather than treating a single attribution map as validation. This sits closer to the small set of interpretable smart-grid forecasting papers \cite{Cifci2025InterpretableSmartGrid} than to the majority of deep load models in the corpus that report no post-hoc attribution at all. The scientific implication is that explainability for graph-temporal energy models must be multi-method and task-specific: stress attributions partially align with engineered OSI components, but demand attributions are not interchangeable across techniques. The remaining gap is the lack of prospective operator studies linking attribution outputs to decision quality; transparency is demonstrated on frozen artefacts, not human-in-the-loop efficacy.

### 9.2.5 Bangladesh smart-grid context and evaluation discipline

The Bangladesh case \cite{Hossain2025BangladeshSmartGrid} and spatial load-shedding literature \cite{Paphitis2025SpatialLoadShedding} provide contextual anchors for interpreting the frozen evaluation record.

Taken together, the empirical programme addresses a geographic and contextual void in the reviewed literature: no collected comparator used Bangladesh division-level smart-grid daily data, and developing-economy national grids remain under-represented in graph-transformer forecasting benchmarks dominated by US and European residential or microgrid settings. The study further departs from much of the shedding literature by forecasting day-ahead regional load and a derived operational stress index for planning visibility rather than optimising real-time load-shedding actions.

Relative to papers with weakly documented splits or metadata-sparse conference reporting, the frozen evaluation chain—chronological hold-out testing, benchmark and ablation suites, inferential testing on the primary demand metric, and locked artefacts—responds to reproducibility concerns identified in the gap analysis without claiming superiority over unbenchmarked external systems. The scientific implication is that rigorous internal benchmarking on a previously unreported national case addresses an important gap identified in the literature even when architectural components are incremental at the operator level. The remaining gap is external validity: conclusions are anchored to the Bangladesh timeline and feature regime; transfer to other developing grids requires new datasets, stress definitions, and independent replication rather than architectural export alone.

## 9.3 Practical Implications

### 9.3.1 Day-ahead operational planning

The completed evaluation shows that the frozen correlation-graph PF-STGT model (S2) delivers the strongest joint demand-and-stress credentials in the benchmark suite on a chronologically held-out test year, with statistically supported improvement over the historical hybrid reference on the primary demand objective and comparatively informative graph-level OSI outputs. For day-ahead operational planning, this may support a single forecasting service that updates regional evening-peak load trajectories and a national stress scalar from the same input window rather than reconciling separate demand and stress indicators after inference.

Operationally, planners could review paired forecasts in a common daily cycle aligned with the one-day horizon and seven-day lookback used in evaluation, which is consistent with existing evening-peak planning rhythms on the national system. Deployment would require adopting the frozen S2 checkpoint and the associated feature and graph artefacts without altering the locked train-only preprocessing rules that governed the reported scores, together with version control that preserves MD5-locked data dependencies referenced in the final results package. A practical caution is that the study does not quantify downstream dispatch, reserve, or shedding outcomes; forecast accuracy improvements documented in Section 8 are planning diagnostics only and should not be read as demonstrated reductions in operational risk or economic cost.

### 9.3.2 Regional monitoring

Regional disaggregation shows that absolute forecast error concentrates in the national load centre while peripheral divisions exhibit comparatively low megawatt deviations, a pattern that persists across model families in the frozen record. Macro-averaged national summaries therefore may understate planning sensitivity in the largest division and may overstate uniform national improvement when division-level offsets are present.

Operationally, regional monitoring should treat the capital division as a separately reported diagnostic alongside equal-weight macro dashboards, with supplementary division tables consulted when prioritising corridors or load centres with the largest residuals. Deployment could facilitate a two-tier reporting layout: a national summary for routine briefings and division-level panels for Dhaka, Khulna, and other high-residual nodes identified in the ablation outputs. A practical caution is that per-region signed-residual distributions and variance diagnostics for S2 are incomplete in the published supplementary materials; monitoring protocols should track division MAE and, where available, audited R² from reference models rather than assuming uniform error character everywhere.

### 9.3.3 Joint demand and OSI forecasting

The study demonstrates that demand-only and stress-optimal configurations occupy different positions in a documented trade-off space: a demand-only variant attains lower megawatt error at the cost of forfeiting OSI outputs, while stress-favouring graph choices can improve OSI fidelity at higher demand error. The selected multi-task configuration is the empirically preferred joint option within the frozen suite, not simultaneous optimum on every task.

Operationally, organisations that require only regional load forecasts may consider a demand-only configuration, whereas control centres that need synchronised load and stress visibility may favour the multi-task S2 pipeline evaluated here. Deployment of the joint model may facilitate paired dashboards for next-day regional demand and graph-level OSI without maintaining two independent training and versioning streams. A practical caution is that OSI performance claims rest primarily on point estimates without stress-specific confidence intervals or hypothesis tests; joint monitoring should treat OSI forecasts as complementary stress indicators to be reviewed alongside demand accuracy, not as validated triggers for automatic control actions.

### 9.3.4 Explainability for operator support

Post-hoc attribution on the frozen model indicates that limitation-stack and calendar coalitions dominate stress explanations, while calendar, lag, and national-generation coalitions feature prominently for the principal load division, with partial agreement between gradient attributions and OSI component drivers across audited case studies. Spatial attribution further concentrates on high-load nodes with moderate alignment between learned attention and the correlation adjacency. Explainability artefacts therefore may support operator review by surfacing which input coalitions co-vary with forecasts, not by proving which physical mechanisms caused outcomes.

Operationally, stress explanation views could foreground limitation and calendar channels, and demand explanation views for the capital region could present calendar and lag coalitions alongside spatial mass on high-load nodes. Deployment could assist trust-building by displaying gradient-based and permutation-based demand attributions separately, consistent with documented disagreement between methods, and by flagging case-study strata where dual-path OSI agreement is absent. A practical caution is that attributions are model-local and partially discordant with component-level OSI decomposition; operators should not treat explanation plots as ground-truth causal maps or as substitutes for engineering judgement on high-stress days where case records show larger single-day OSI gaps.

### 9.3.5 Deployment considerations

The frozen evaluation programme—benchmark comparison, ablation and architecture selection, error geography, and attribution analysis on locked artefacts—provides a reproducible evidence base for considering S2, but it does not constitute a field deployment trial. Integration would require the chronological feature store, correlation adjacency, W20 multi-task checkpoint, and inference pipeline consistent with the experimental setup, together with monitoring against published macro and division-level accuracy thresholds and periodic retraining policy aligned with the chronological split discipline used in evaluation.

Operationally, shadow-mode forecasting alongside incumbent tools before any operational cutover is a recommended deployment strategy that the present study did not evaluate; such a prudent rollout may support division-level and stress-case review built into acceptance checks. Deployment planning should budget for ongoing monitoring of limitation and calendar inputs highlighted in error and attribution analyses, and for separate Dhaka reporting given persistent error concentration. Operational guidance should distinguish macro national summaries from division diagnostics and from explanation outputs that use different attribution methods. A practical caution is that absence of complete S2 residual time-series diagnostics and forecast uncertainty bounds in the current evaluation limits automated bias alarms; human oversight remains necessary until extended error-analysis protocols are executed and operator-facing validation is conducted beyond the frozen test record.

## 9.4 Limitations

### 9.4.1 Dataset scope

The empirical evidence is bounded by a single chronologically split daily record of the Bangladesh national power system: nine administrative divisions, a seven-day input window, a one-day evening-peak forecast horizon, and a feature regime comprising nine node-level and seventeen global channels derived from the frozen preprocessing pipeline. That design supports reproducible within-dataset comparison but does not span alternative temporal resolutions, intra-day dispatch horizons, or feature sets outside the locked parquet artefacts.

The limitation arises because model selection, graph construction, and OSI definition were calibrated on one national timeline (train through mid-2023, validation through early 2024, test on 264 windows through late 2024) with warm-up rules and MD5-locked inputs fixed before publication freeze. Interpretation of accuracy rankings, ablation outcomes, and attribution patterns therefore applies to this feature geometry and calendar span rather than to arbitrary grid telemetry schemas or longer institutional archives. Conclusions on joint demand-and-stress forecasting are scoped to the documented Bangladesh feature store and chronological split; they do not establish performance under different data vintages, missing-data regimes, or revised stress formulations without re-execution of the frozen evaluation chain.

### 9.4.2 Bangladesh-only evaluation

All reported benchmarks, ablations, error characterisations, and explainability outputs derive from the Bangladesh national grid case alone; no independent replication on other South Asian or developing-economy systems is included in the frozen record. The study addresses a geographic void in the reviewed literature but remains a single-country evidence base.

National grids differ in topology, limitation reporting, shedding practice, and divisional aggregation; correlation-graph priors and limitation-stack coalitions that perform well here were estimated from Bangladesh operational records and may not transfer where coupling structure or stress semantics differ. Interpretation of graph-topology findings, multi-task trade-offs, and regional error geography is therefore country-specific: external validity is limited to systems with comparable divisional structure, limitation visibility, and daily planning cadence. Claims about graph-transformer multi-task feasibility extend to the evaluated Bangladesh setting; they do not constitute cross-national generalisation without new datasets and independent validation.

### 9.4.3 Benchmark scope

Comparative evidence is confined to the frozen internal suite—seven reference models (B01–B07) plus the final proposed configuration S2—and six ablation variants (A1–A6) drawn from the same PF-STGT family and training protocol. No external state-of-the-art leaderboard, proprietary operator model, or real-time control benchmark participates in the reported rankings.

Inferential testing is defined primarily on per-window macro demand MAE: Wilcoxon comparisons and Bonferroni-adjusted significance apply to demand error, with direct S2-versus–random-forest testing absent from the statistical record. Classical ensembles exhibit favourable macro R² without leading on megawatt MAE, and a demand-only ablation (A4) attains lower demand error than the selected multi-task configuration, documenting an explicit demand–stress trade-off within the suite rather than universal superiority on every metric. Interpretation of “leading” performance is therefore bounded to the frozen benchmark and ablation tables on the primary demand objective and reported OSI point estimates; it does not assert dominance over unbenchmarked external systems or simultaneous optimality on demand and stress across all configuration rows.

### 9.4.4 Uncertainty estimation

The evaluation reports point forecasts and aggregate error metrics—MAE, RMSE, R², and MAPE on demand; MAE and R² on OSI—without prediction intervals, ensemble spread, conformal bounds, or other uncertainty quantification on the held-out test split. Bootstrap confidence intervals exist for selected paired demand MAE contrasts but do not cover OSI residuals or per-division S2 forecasts.

Stress-specific hypothesis tests, multiple-comparison correction on OSI metrics, and pooled signed-residual diagnostics for the final checkpoint were not executed in the frozen results package; temporal error segmentation defined in the error-analysis protocol likewise remains unreported for S2. Interpretation of OSI gains and regional error structure therefore rests on point estimates and association summaries rather than on distributional or interval-based risk statements. Supported demand improvements over the historical hybrid reference retain inferential backing; stress accuracy, bias characterisation, and forecast confidence for deployment monitoring must be read with the narrower inferential and diagnostic coverage documented in Section 8.

### 9.4.5 Explainability limits

Post-hoc attribution was applied to the frozen S2 checkpoint using grouped integrated gradients, permutation importance, attention export, and stratified case studies; these methods describe model behaviour on the evaluation sample and do not establish physical causation or operator-ground-truth driver validation. Global gradient attributions for demand were computed on twenty validation windows; dual-path OSI comparison draws on twenty-four stratified days, of which only four lie on the test split.

Cross-method agreement is partial: OSI component drivers align with the top gradient-attribution coalition in 52.2% of audited case studies, demand gradient and permutation rankings disagree (Spearman ρ = −0.564), and spatial attention correlates moderately with the correlation adjacency (ρ = 0.422). Near-uniform temporal attention weights further limit claims of sharp lag selectivity from attribution outputs alone. Interpretation of explainability artefacts is limited to transparency into the frozen model’s input sensitivities; they do not validate OSI physics, confirm feature rankings across methods, or substitute for engineering judgement on high-stress days where dual-path records diverge. Explainability conclusions are scoped to the documented multi-method discordance and sample sizes in Table 7.

### 9.4.6 Deployment limits

The completed programme is an offline, chronologically held-out evaluation on locked artefacts; it does not include field deployment, operator-in-the-loop trials, shadow-mode operation, or quantification of downstream dispatch, reserve, shedding, or economic outcomes. Production integration would depend on reproducing the frozen feature store, correlation adjacency, W20 multi-task checkpoint, and inference pipeline under the same train-only preprocessing rules that governed reported scores.

Single-seed training (seed 42), incomplete S2 residual time-series stores, and absence of forecast uncertainty bounds constrain operational readiness assessment to accuracy diagnostics published in the frozen tables rather than to validated runtime monitoring or cutover evidence. Interpretation of practical implications in Section 9.3 is advisory: deployment strategies discussed there are recommendations grounded in evaluation gaps, not outcomes measured in the present study. The boundary of the reported evidence is reproducible forecasting performance and post-hoc analysis on the Bangladesh test year; operational adoption, reliability effects, and cost consequences lie outside the scope of the frozen evaluation record.

## 9.5 Future Research Directions

### 9.5.1 External validation

The completed study establishes that correlation-graph PF-STGT multi-task learning can jointly forecast regional evening-peak demand and a graph-level Operational Stress Index on a chronologically held-out year of Bangladesh division-level data, with statistically supported demand improvement over the historical hybrid reference within the frozen benchmark suite. That evidence is internally rigorous but geographically singular.

Developing-economy national grids differ in divisional aggregation, limitation reporting, and inter-regional coupling; the correlation prior and OSI semantics calibrated here may not transfer without re-estimation on independent records. Future studies could replicate the frozen evaluation protocol—chronological splitting, joint demand–stress targets, ablation of graph priors, and multi-method attribution—on comparable South Asian or similarly structured national systems with documented operational stress indicators. Further investigation may compare whether correlation-graph advantage persists where geographical coupling is weaker or limitation dynamics are reported differently.

Such external validation would separate architecture-specific gains from country-specific feature geometry, strengthening or qualifying the generalisability of data-driven graph priors for national-scale energy forecasting. The scientific value lies in converting a single-country demonstration into cross-context evidence on when joint load-and-stress graph-transformer forecasting remains viable outside the Bangladesh timeline.

### 9.5.2 Richer graph construction

Ablation evidence shows that correlation-only adjacency outperforms geographical-only and hybrid priors for joint demand and stress performance on this dataset, while a geographical graph variant attains superior OSI point estimates at materially higher demand error, documenting a demand–stress trade-off rather than a single optimal topology. Transformer capacity further appears conditional on graph choice: removal on the correlation graph degrades demand performance sharply, whereas hybrid-topology simplification does not yield statistically distinguishable demand change.

The frozen suite evaluates static correlation (τ = 0.65), geographical, and hybrid adjacencies but does not explore dynamic, multi-relational, or jointly learned graph structures that could encode time-varying coupling or fuse infrastructure topology with data-driven edges. An important direction is comparative evaluation of adaptive correlation graphs, multi-graph attention, and physics-informed edge constraints under the same multi-task objective and chronological discipline used here. Further investigation may examine whether richer priors can narrow the documented gap between stress-favouring and demand-favouring configurations without reverting to the hybrid reference design.

Advancing graph construction beyond the frozen ablation grid would clarify whether correlation-graph simplification represents a robust national-grid design principle or a Bangladesh-specific optimum. The scientific value is a more complete map of graph–task interactions in multi-node operational forecasting, informing prior selection where limitation dynamics and load coupling co-evolve.

### 9.5.3 Uncertainty-aware forecasting

Demand-side inferential support in the frozen record includes paired Wilcoxon tests and bootstrap confidence intervals on macro MAE contrasts, whereas OSI performance is reported through point estimates without stress-specific hypothesis tests, prediction intervals, or pooled signed-residual diagnostics for the final checkpoint. Planners reviewing joint forecasts therefore lack distributional statements on per-division error and stress uncertainty on the held-out split.

Probabilistic forecasting—through deep ensembles, quantile heads, conformal calibration, or Bayesian approximations—could extend the present point-forecast pipeline to supply interval-valued demand and OSI outputs aligned with the same chronological evaluation framework. Complementary work may execute the predefined residual and temporal error protocols on the frozen S2 checkpoint, including signed-bias characterisation and regime-conditioned error segments, together with inferential testing on OSI residuals where sample design permits. Further investigation may assess whether uncertainty bands improve discrimination on high-stress test days where case records show larger single-day OSI gaps.

Uncertainty-aware extensions would shift interpretation from accuracy rankings alone toward risk-aware day-ahead planning metrics. The scientific value is coupling the demonstrated joint forecasting capability with statistically auditable confidence statements suitable for operational monitoring and automated bias detection.

### 9.5.4 Explainability validation

Post-hoc attribution on the frozen model surfaces coherent coalition-level and spatial patterns—limitation and calendar structure for stress, calendar and lag channels for the principal load division—with explicit documentation of partial dual-path agreement against OSI component drivers and discordance between gradient-based and permutation-based demand rankings. Transparency is demonstrated on frozen artefacts rather than through prospective validation with domain experts.

Attribution samples are modest relative to the full test split, and explainability methods disagree on demand feature order; neither outcome establishes that explanation outputs improve operator decisions or align with ground-truth causal structure on high-stress days. Future studies could expand attribution coverage across held-out test windows, standardise multi-method reporting templates for dual-task dashboards, and conduct controlled operator studies that measure whether discordant explanation views affect forecast trust or review behaviour. Further investigation may compare attribution outputs against independent engineering assessments of limitation events and reserve conditions on stratified stress cases.

Prospective explainability validation would bridge post-hoc model transparency and operational decision quality. The scientific value is empirical evidence on which attribution families are stable, task-specific, and useful for human review in national graph-transformer forecasting settings where method disagreement is already documented.

### 9.5.5 Deployment studies

The frozen evaluation programme delivers reproducible offline accuracy diagnostics, architecture selection, and post-hoc analysis on locked data artefacts, but it does not measure runtime integration, shadow-mode operation, data-drift response, or downstream planning outcomes. Practical implications in Section 9.3 therefore remain conditional on evidence not collected in the present study.

Translating benchmark performance into operational utility requires field-oriented study designs that preserve the chronological and preprocessing discipline of the frozen pipeline while exposing the model to production data feeds, incumbent forecast comparisons, and division-level monitoring workflows. Future studies could implement shadow-mode forecasting alongside existing tools, document retraining triggers under extending timelines, and record operator review of paired demand–OSI dashboards under realistic planning cycles. Further investigation may quantify whether published macro and division-level error thresholds remain stable when feature distributions shift beyond the 2024 test year.

Deployment-oriented research would connect forecasting accuracy to observable operational review practices without substituting for the present offline evidence base. The scientific value is closing the evidentiary loop between reproducible model evaluation and documented behaviour under conditions that approximate control-centre use, while maintaining the boundary between planning diagnostics and unmeasured reliability or economic outcomes.
