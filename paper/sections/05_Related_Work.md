# 5 Related Work

This section surveys prior research along four themes that bound the present study: short-horizon load forecasting, graph-based spatio-temporal modelling, multi-task learning in energy systems, and explainable artificial intelligence for forecasting. The synthesis draws on a structured critical review of fifty-five peer-reviewed and preprint studies collected for the Bangladesh smart-grid forecasting programme (2023–2026), supplemented by limitation and opportunity catalogues derived from reviewer-style analysis of that corpus. The objective is not to enumerate individual publications, but to compare methodologies, datasets, assumptions, limitations, and unresolved challenges that collectively define the research space addressed by the Correlation-Aware Multi-Task Forecasting Framework implemented as PF-STGT.

## 5.1 Load Forecasting

Short-horizon electricity load forecasting remains the largest thematic cluster in the reviewed literature. Methods span classical statistical and machine-learning regressors through deep recurrent, convolutional, and transformer architectures applied primarily to scalar or multivariate load series. A substantial share of recent work targets residential or advanced-metering-infrastructure (AMI) consumption at sub-daily or daily resolution, often assuming dense sampling, homogeneous prosumers, or building-level aggregates rather than administratively defined regional nodes on a national transmission footprint. Ensemble tree methods, gradient boosting, and hybrid statistical–deep pipelines are frequently reported as strong tabular baselines on these datasets \cite{Breiman2001RandomForest,Chen2016XGBoost,Musbah2025LoadForecasting}, reflecting long-standing evidence that well-tuned non-graphical models remain competitive when spatial structure is implicit in feature engineering rather than explicit in the model architecture.

Deep learning contributions in the load-forecasting cluster emphasise temporal pattern extraction: stacked recurrent encoders, convolutional front-ends, Autoformer- and transformer-style sequence models \cite{Hochreiter1997LSTM,Wu2021Autoformer,Helmy2024AutoformerEV,Guillen2026LoadComposition}, and multi-encoder designs that fuse textual or exogenous embeddings with numerical load history. Common assumptions include stationary seasonality within the training window, availability of long uninterrupted series, and forecast horizons aligned to market or building-management cadences that may differ from daily national operational reporting. Industrial and sector-specific case studies occasionally use public national statistics—such as industrial electricity accounts in North American datasets—but developing-economy national grids with division-level evening-peak reporting and co-visible limitation indicators are effectively absent from the collected corpus. Comparative studies that benchmark many deep architectures on common splits provide useful methodological guidance on model families \cite{Musbah2025LoadForecasting}, yet they seldom incorporate spatial graph structure or operational stress targets and therefore address only part of the planning problem faced by national-system operators.

A parallel literature stream addresses load shedding, under-frequency response, and emergency control rather than ex-ante day-ahead forecasting for planning. Approximately one quarter of the reviewed papers belong to this shedding and control cluster \cite{Paphitis2025SpatialLoadShedding,Wang2026SHAPLoadShedding}. Their objectives are optimising shedding sequences, preserving voltage or frequency stability, coordinating generator tripping, or managing islanded microgrids under disturbance. Datasets are often simulation-based, event-centric, or tied to specific subsystems and islanding scenarios. Methodologically, these studies employ particle-swarm and other metaheuristic optimisers, reinforcement learning, decentralised control rules, and predictive components that serve real-time actuation rather than graded daily stress visibility. The distinction matters for task formulation: forecasting sparse or zero-inflated regional unmet-demand signals for planning is methodologically adjacent to shedding research but not equivalent to optimising control actions at disturbance timescales. Operational stress and reliability studies in the corpus address adjacent but distinct questions—transmission-level security assessment, semi-Markov reliability models, and vulnerability of demand-response schemes under cyber threat—but do not typically produce daily forecastable stress indices aligned with divisional demand series on national operational timelines.

Across the load-forecasting cluster, recurring limitations include weak external-validity reporting—particularly the absence of South Asian or Bangladesh division-level case studies—metadata-sparse conference abstracts that preclude reproducible dataset characterisation, and evaluation metrics that mix pooled and macro-aggregated definitions of coefficient of determination without explicit documentation. Unresolved challenges include adapting dense AMI-centric pipelines to daily national operational timelines with calendar gaps, modelling regional heterogeneity on footprints where a single division dominates the load budget, and coupling load forecasts with system-pressure indicators derived from limitation dynamics rather than treating shedding as a control output alone.

## 5.2 Graph-based Spatio-Temporal Forecasting

Graph neural networks and graph-attention architectures \cite{Kipf2017GCN,Sun2024GLFN} constitute a smaller but methodologically central subset of the reviewed corpus. Reported graph-based load models typically construct nodes from geographic areas, charging stations, integrated-energy-system zones, or residential feeders, and define edges through administrative proximity, infrastructure topology, or correlation in historical series. Spatio-temporal graph convolutional networks propagate information across fixed neighbourhoods at each timestep before or after temporal encoding; multi-graph and attention-based variants allow multiple edge types or learned weighting among neighbours. High-relevance comparators in the corpus combine graph structure with temporal convolution or attention for EV charging load, integrated multi-energy systems, ISO-level system operators, and residential transfer-learning settings \cite{Shi2024EVChargingGNN,Zhuang2024MultiScaleGNN,Wu2023TransferLearningGNN}.

Transformer-based spatio-temporal forecasting appears in a related subset of papers that either stack graph attention with temporal transformers or apply dual-channel spatial–temporal transformers to decoupled seasonal trends \cite{Zhao2024GraphAttentionTransformer,Bentsen2023GraphTransformer,Vaswani2017Attention}. These methods assume that explicit relational structure improves forecasts when nodes exhibit measurable coupling, and they often evaluate on US or European residential benchmarks, wind-speed graphs, or charging-network topologies with qualitatively different spatial semantics from a nine-division national grid. Geographic adjacency is a common inductive bias: border-connected regions receive uniform or topology-derived edge weights. Data-driven correlation graphs appear less frequently as the sole spatial prior, and hybrid geographic–correlation constructions are seldom ablated against correlation-only alternatives on developing-economy operational records.

Dataset assumptions in the graph-temporal cluster diverge markedly from the Bangladesh setting motivating the present work. Node counts range from small residential subgraphs to multi-energy system graphs; temporal resolution is often sub-hourly; and stress or shedding targets are rarely co-forecast with regional demand in the same graph-transformer pipeline. Shallow graph-convolution baselines without transformer capacity represent an important methodological floor in the broader literature \cite{Yu2018STGCN,Zhao2020TGCN}: they test whether graph propagation alone suffices when temporal encoding is limited, and they highlight that graph conditioning is not automatically sufficient when the temporal branch cannot represent multi-day context on low-frequency daily series. Limitations recorded in the critical review include insufficient reporting of graph-construction splits—whether adjacency is estimated on training data only—sparse explainability of learned spatial weights, and limited evidence on when geographical priors help versus add noise on strongly correlated national demand series.

Unresolved challenges in graph-based spatio-temporal forecasting for power systems include selecting graph priors when inter-regional coupling is driven by shared national growth and seasonality rather than border adjacency alone; determining whether transformer capacity is uniformly necessary or conditional on graph topology; and extending graph-temporal models from residential or charging-network benchmarks to national division-level nodes with synchronised limitation covariates. None of the collected high-relevance graph or transformer studies address Bangladesh shedding context or daily OSI-style stress targets, leaving geographic and task-formulation gaps at the intersection of spatial coupling and operational constraint forecasting.

## 5.3 Multi-Task Learning

Multi-task learning in energy systems \cite{Caruana1997Multitask} occupies a narrow band of the reviewed literature relative to single-task load forecasting and shedding control. Reported formulations most often couple related energy vectors—electricity, heat, gas—or hierarchical multi-step horizons within a single sector, using shared encoders and task-specific heads with attention-based fusion. One hierarchical multi-energy study in the corpus applies multi-task structure to integrated energy system load forecasting with spatio-temporal attention \cite{Song2024MultiTaskEnergy,Zhuang2024MultiScaleGNN}, illustrating that shared representations can exploit cross-vector structure when tasks are statistically related. However, explicit coupling of continuous multi-node regional demand with a daily operational stress index derived from shedding intensity, reserve margin, and limitation pressure is not represented among the collected comparators.

Task isolation remains the dominant pattern outside this small multi-task subset. Demand forecasting papers treat load as the sole supervised target; shedding papers optimise control or classify events; operational-stress and reliability studies focus on transmission assets, semi-Markov outage models, or security assessment functionals rather than forecastable daily stress scalars for dispatch planning. Where auxiliary targets appear—such as controllability scores or composition forecasts—they are not paired with graph-level spatial encoders on national regional footprints. The reviewed opportunity catalogue records comparatively few explicit multi-task extension proposals, suggesting that joint demand-and-stress formulation is recognised as conceptually attractive but under-implemented in published graph-temporal energy models.

Methodological assumptions in multi-task energy learning include that shared trunks improve data efficiency when tasks share latent structure, and that loss weighting can be tuned to mitigate task interference. The literature provides limited guidance on balancing megawatt-scale robust regression against bounded stress targets in daily operational data, or on repairing stress-head collapse when naive multi-task weighting yields uninformative auxiliary outputs—issues that arise when stress labels are derived composites rather than dense shedding events. Sparse and zero-inflated regional load signals further complicate direct multi-target regression of shedding at the node level, motivating graph-level stress indices that aggregate system pressure without treating every division as a shedding classifier.

Limitations of the multi-task strand include scarce ablation of single-task versus multi-task ceilings on identical architectures, infrequent reporting of Pareto trade-offs when auxiliary tasks marginally degrade primary metrics, and absence of developing-economy national case studies with documented chronological splits. Unresolved challenges include determining when multi-task learning is preferable to separate pipelines for operators who require only demand or only stress visibility, calibrating task weights under class imbalance and composite-target semantics, and evaluating whether shared graph-temporal encoders transfer stress-relevant structure beyond what single-task models extract from global limitation covariates alone.

## 5.4 Explainable AI

Explainability for load and smart-grid forecasting is sparsely represented in the reviewed corpus \cite{Baur2024XAIReview}. Only a small fraction of collected papers report post-hoc or interpretable-by-design explanations; the majority of deep forecasting models provide no attribution analysis. Where explainability appears, it clusters into three methodological styles: SHAP or Shapley-value attributions applied to control or shedding policies—often in microgrid under-frequency settings with knowledge distillation \cite{Wang2026SHAPLoadShedding,Lundberg2017SHAP}; general interpretable-machine-learning frameworks for decentralised smart-grid prediction \cite{Cifci2025InterpretableSmartGrid}; and review articles cataloguing feature-importance, SHAP, and partial-dependence practices for scalar load series without graph-temporal multi-task outputs \cite{Baur2024XAIReview}.

Assumptions in the explainability literature often treat attribution as a supplement to control validation or consumer-facing interpretability rather than as operator-facing diagnostics for coupled regional demand and national stress forecasts. Graph-temporal models rarely export spatial attention, coalition-grouped gradients, and dual-path comparison against engineered stress components in a single evaluation programme. Review-level work \cite{Baur2024XAIReview} acknowledges that explainability methods can disagree on feature rankings for time-series models, yet few empirical studies document that disagreement explicitly for multi-node graph transformers or report agreement between gradient attributions and permutation-based importance on megawatt demand targets.

Limitations include attributions evaluated on small validation samples without prospective operator studies, conflation of post-hoc sensitivity with physical causation, and explainability reported for single-task models while stress and demand explanations are not jointly audited against domain-decomposed indices. Review-level surveys note that method choice materially affects ranked drivers for load forecasts, reinforcing the need for multi-method reporting rather than single-map validation when models feed operator review. Unresolved challenges involve standardising multi-method attribution for dual-task dashboards, aligning model explanations with engineered stress components when composite targets mix shedding, reserve, and limitation channels, and extending microgrid-oriented SHAP workflows to national graph models with eleven feature coalitions and division-level node mass concentration.

## 5.5 Summary and Research Gap

Taken together, the reviewed literature establishes capable methods within each thematic silo—tabular and deep load forecasters on residential or industrial series, graph and transformer models on topology-defined networks, hierarchical multi-energy multi-task encoders, and isolated explainability studies—but seldom integrates them on the problem geometry addressed here. Four cross-cutting gaps emerge from the evidence-based gap matrix and critical review.

First, geographic and contextual validity is limited: no collected peer-reviewed spatio-temporal load or shedding forecasting study uses Bangladesh division-level smart-grid daily data \cite{Hossain2025BangladeshSmartGrid}, and developing-economy national grids remain under-represented relative to US and European AMI-centric benchmarks. Second, task formulation remains fragmented: continuous regional demand forecasting is rarely coupled with operational-stress-style targets in one multi-task graph-temporal model, while shedding research predominantly targets control and optimisation rather than day-ahead forecast-centric planning visibility. Third, methodological integration is incomplete: only a minority of papers combine graph-based spatial coupling with transformer-style temporal modelling for multi-node forecasting, and few co-design limitation-aware exogenous covariates—fuel, water, maintenance, temperature anomalies, and calendar structure—with spatio-temporal architectures or document chronological evaluation discipline with ablations and corrected inferential testing. Fourth, explainability is rarely embedded in coupled demand-and-stress graph forecasts with explicit multi-method reporting.

These gaps motivate the Correlation-Aware Multi-Task Forecasting Framework (PF-STGT) as a response aligned with the identified voids rather than as an assertion of universal superiority over prior art. The framework targets joint next-day regional evening-peak demand and graph-level Operational Stress Index prediction on Bangladesh national operational records under leakage-safe chronological evaluation. It adopts a train-estimated correlation graph as an empirically testable spatial prior alternative to geography-first or hybrid constructions common in the literature, pairs graph-aware and temporal transformer encoding in a parallel-fusion architecture suited to multi-node daily windows, and applies an explicitly repaired multi-task loss formulation so that stress learning remains stable alongside megawatt demand regression. Post-hoc attribution is integrated as a reporting obligation—coalition-level gradients, permutation comparisons, attention export, and case-stratified dual-path stress review—rather than as an optional appendix, reflecting the corpus-wide shortage of explainability in graph-temporal energy forecasting.

The present study therefore occupies the intersection of four under-served areas in the reviewed literature: Bangladesh division-level smart-grid analytics, multi-task coupling of demand and operational stress, graph-temporal modelling with documented graph-prior ablation, and explainability analysis on frozen multi-task outputs. It does not claim to resolve every unresolved challenge identified above—external replication on other national grids, probabilistic uncertainty quantification, and prospective operator validation remain outside the scope of a single case study—but it provides a reproducible, evidence-locked foundation on which those questions can be examined.
