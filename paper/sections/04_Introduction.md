# 1 Introduction

Electricity system operators in national grids must anticipate regional load and assess system pressure on a day-ahead horizon to coordinate generation dispatch, reserve planning, and constraint management. In developing economies, where supply limitations, infrastructure stress, and intermittent load shedding can coincide with rapid demand growth, planning visibility depends on timely forecasts that reflect both where load will concentrate across administrative regions and how severely operational constraints may bind at the system level. Unlike highly instrumented transmission networks with dense phasor measurement coverage, many national systems in South Asia report operational state through daily aggregated division-level balances, national generation scalars, and qualitative limitation indicators that nevertheless encode recurring patterns of stress when reserves tighten or fuel and infrastructure constraints bind. Historical operational records from smart-grid reporting platforms increasingly offer daily traces of division-level demand, supply shortfalls, fuel and maintenance limitations, and national generation context. These records create an opportunity to move beyond aggregate national load models toward spatio-temporal learning approaches that respect inter-regional coupling while incorporating system-wide stress signals relevant to control-centre review. For the Bangladesh national power system, daily operational data are reported across nine administrative divisions over a multi-year timeline, providing a documented case in which regional evening-peak demand and limitation dynamics are co-visible within each daily record \cite{Hossain2025BangladeshSmartGrid}. Accurate short-horizon forecasting in such settings is not merely a statistical exercise: planners require forecasts that are spatially disaggregated, temporally aligned to the operational reporting cadence, and interpretable enough to support human review when stress indicators and load trajectories must be judged together.

Despite the availability of rich operational timelines, forecasting practice in power systems often treats load prediction and stress monitoring as separate analytical threads. Classical and ensemble machine-learning models \cite{Breiman2001RandomForest,Chen2016XGBoost,Musbah2025LoadForecasting} typically forecast demand at regional or national scale without an explicit graph structure to encode coupling among divisions, while shallow recurrent or graph-convolution architectures \cite{Hochreiter1997LSTM,Yu2018STGCN} may struggle to combine long-range temporal context with informative multi-node representations on national footprints. Graph-based methods \cite{Kipf2017GCN,Wu2023TransferLearningGNN,Shi2024EVChargingGNN} imported from residential, microgrid, or infrastructure-topology settings frequently rely on geographical adjacency or fixed network maps that may not capture data-driven co-movement among regional loads in developing-economy grids. At the same time, operational stress—arising from reserve shortfall, aggregated limitation pressure, and shedding intensity—is seldom forecast jointly with regional demand in a single multi-task model; shedding-oriented studies in the literature \cite{Paphitis2025SpatialLoadShedding,Wang2026SHAPLoadShedding} more often address control, optimisation, or under-frequency response than day-ahead forecasting of graded stress indices for planning visibility. Explainability, where present \cite{Baur2024XAIReview,Cifci2025InterpretableSmartGrid}, is rarely integrated with multi-node graph-temporal forecasts that emit coupled load and stress outputs for operator-facing review. The result is a persistent mismatch between what operational records contain—synchronised regional load and system constraint context—and what many forecasting pipelines deliver: disconnected single-task predictions with limited spatial structure and limited transparency.

The present study addresses that mismatch by formulating day-ahead forecasting as a supervised multi-task problem \cite{Caruana1997Multitask} on a fixed set of spatial units. The primary task is next-day regional evening-peak demand across nine divisions; the secondary task is a graph-level Operational Stress Index (OSI) that summarises system-wide pressure from shedding intensity, generation reserve margin, and operational limitation components normalised under a leakage-safe training policy. OSI is constructed as a bounded composite score rather than a sparse per-division shedding label, preserving graded stress magnitude on days when reserve or limitation pressure is elevated even if shedding is absent. The two targets are operationally related yet statistically non-redundant: demand describes expected load levels by region, whereas OSI encodes constraint-related pressure that is not fully determined by demand alone. Joint estimation from shared spatio-temporal inputs is motivated by the need for synchronised day-ahead visibility rather than by treating stress prediction as an incidental add-on to load modelling. The research problem therefore asks whether a single spatio-temporal deep learning framework can learn coupled regional demand and operational stress forecasts from historical Bangladesh grid records under chronological evaluation discipline, and which design choices—graph prior, architectural components, task weighting, and post-hoc attribution—are empirically justified on this national case.

A structured review of the collected literature and an evidence-based gap analysis identify several limitations that motivate the present work without duplicating a full survey here. First, peer-reviewed spatio-temporal load and shedding studies rarely use Bangladesh division-level smart-grid daily data; developing-economy national grids remain under-represented relative to US and European residential or microgrid benchmarks. Second, continuous multi-node demand forecasting is seldom coupled with operational-stress-style targets in one multi-task framework; task isolation between load and shedding persists in much of the corpus. Third, shedding research often targets control actions rather than forecast-centric formulations of graded daily stress suitable for planning. Fourth, only a minority of recent work combines graph-based spatial coupling with transformer-style temporal modelling \cite{Zhao2024GraphAttentionTransformer,Bentsen2023GraphTransformer,Vaswani2017Attention} for multi-node national forecasting, and none of the collected comparators address the Bangladesh shedding and limitation context directly. Fifth, explainable artificial intelligence appears in a small fraction of graph-temporal energy studies \cite{Baur2024XAIReview,Lundberg2017SHAP} and is seldom reported with explicit multi-method discordance for coupled demand and stress outputs. Sixth, operational stress assessment in adjacent literature frequently emphasises transmission or microgrid assets rather than daily regional stress indices derived from demand–supply–limitation dynamics visible in national operational reports. Seventh, exogenous limitation covariates—fuel, water, maintenance, and calendar structure—are not consistently co-designed with spatio-temporal architectures in published load models. Eighth, weak documentation of chronological splits, baselines, and ablations in part of the conference literature complicates fair comparison of proposed methods. Together, these gaps define the scope of contribution: a reproducible, evidence-locked study on a previously unreported national case that couples graph-temporal learning, multi-task stress forecasting, and operator-oriented attribution within one frozen evaluation programme.

To respond to that scope, this paper proposes the Correlation-Aware Multi-Task Forecasting Framework, implemented as a Parallel-Fusion Spatio-Temporal Graph Transformer (PF-STGT). The framework ingests a fixed lookback window of node-level and national context features for each administrative division, together with a train-estimated correlation graph that encodes inter-regional demand coupling from training-partition data only. A parallel dual-branch encoder combines graph-aware spatial attention with temporal transformer capacity \cite{Vaswani2017Attention,Kipf2017GCN} over the lookback window; learned fusion feeds shared representations into task-specific heads that decode regional megawatt demand and a bounded system-wide OSI scalar for the next operating day. Graph construction evaluates geographical, hybrid, and correlation-only priors; the proposed configuration adopts correlation-thresholded adjacency as the definitive spatial prior following empirical comparison rather than assuming that geographical proximity alone captures predictive coupling on this dataset. Multi-task training employs a scale-aware joint loss with explicit stress weighting \cite{Caruana1997Multitask,Song2024MultiTaskEnergy} so that shared representations encode limitation-relevant structure without collapsing to demand-only learning—a design requirement identified during protocol repair when unconstrained multi-task optimisation failed to produce informative stress outputs. Post-hoc attribution applies grouped integrated gradients, permutation importance, spatial and temporal attention export \cite{Sundararajan2017IntegratedGradients,Breiman2001RandomForest,Lundberg2017SHAP}, and stratified case studies on the frozen final checkpoint to supply coalition-level, spatial, and dual-path stress explanations for operator review, with method disagreement reported explicitly rather than treated as validation of physical mechanisms. Inputs combine engineered autoregressive, calendar, limitation, and grid-aggregate features with a seven-day lookback aligned to the daily reporting cadence of the source timeline. The framework is evaluated on a chronologically partitioned timeline with locked data artefacts, internal benchmarks spanning classical, recurrent, and graph-convolution references \cite{Breiman2001RandomForest,Hochreiter1997LSTM,Zhao2020TGCN}, configuration ablations, architecture simplification, error geography, and explainability analysis; detailed protocol and implementation specifications are deferred to later sections.

The principal contributions of this study are as follows. First, a Correlation-Aware Multi-Task Forecasting Framework (final configuration S2) is introduced for joint day-ahead regional demand and graph-level OSI prediction from a single spatio-temporal graph-transformer model on Bangladesh national grid data. Second, an evidence-driven architecture selection process replaces the initial hybrid geographical–correlation graph design with a correlation-only adjacency prior while retaining full temporal encoder capacity where ablation indicates it is consequential for the selected topology. Third, a repaired multi-task training protocol with explicit demand–stress loss balancing enables stable joint learning of load and stress targets under the frozen W20 specification. Fourth, a rigorous empirical evaluation programme—comprising external benchmarks, component ablations, inferential testing on the primary demand metric with multiple-comparison correction, and metric verification for classical baselines—supports reproducible comparison within the frozen suite. Fifth, an integrated explainability analysis on the final model supplies multi-method attribution artefacts for both forecasting tasks, including spatial and temporal views and comparison against OSI component decomposition on audited case studies. These contributions are established through offline evaluation on held-out test data; they are positioned as planning and transparency evidence rather than as proof of deployment outcomes or universal architectural superiority.
