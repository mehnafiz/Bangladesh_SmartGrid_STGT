## 8.1 Overall Benchmark Performance

### 8.1.1 Overall benchmark summary

The benchmark evaluation compares the proposed correlation-graph PF-STGT model (S2) against seven reference models (B01–B07) on the held-out test split comprising 264 forecast windows. **Table 3** consolidates macro-averaged demand metrics over nine administrative divisions together with graph-level Operational Stress Index (OSI) metrics where available. The primary ranking criterion is test macro demand mean absolute error (MAE) in megawatts.

Among all entries in Table 3, S2 attains the lowest demand MAE at 88.65 MW, followed by the historical hybrid-graph PF-STGT reference B07 at 93.31 MW and the random forest baseline B02 at 97.03 MW. At the opposite end, the temporal graph convolutional baseline B06 records the highest demand MAE at 257.21 MW, with linear regression B01 close behind at 247.79 MW. This separation indicates that the proposed multi-task graph-transformer configuration occupies the leading position on the primary metric within the frozen benchmark suite, while the recurrent baselines B04 (LSTM) and B05 (GRU) and the graph-convolution baseline B06 (T-GCN) \cite{Zhao2020TGCN} remain far above the competitive tier on megawatt MAE. For day-ahead national dispatch planning, the relevant takeaway is that the final proposed model delivers the smallest average absolute regional forecast error among all benchmarked learners on the 2024 test period.

### 8.1.2 Demand forecasting performance

The lowest macro demand MAE in Table 3 belongs to S2 (88.65 MW; RMSE 127.29 MW; MAPE 6.55%). B07, the prior PF-STGT W20 hybrid reference, records 93.31 MW MAE with RMSE 128.81 MW and MAPE 6.76%. The strongest classical competitor, random forest B02 \cite{Breiman2001RandomForest}, reaches 97.03 MW MAE with RMSE 156.99 MW, while gradient boosting B03 \cite{Chen2016XGBoost} yields 109.73 MW MAE. These three models form a distinct upper tier in which graph-aware or ensemble tabular methods outperform the remaining candidates by a wide margin.

Recurrent baselines without explicit graph structure perform poorly: B04 (LSTM) \cite{Hochreiter1997LSTM} and B05 (GRU) \cite{Cho2014GRU} achieve demand MAE of 237.03 MW and 233.48 MW respectively, with negative macro R² values (−0.242 and −0.201). The graph-based but non-transformer baseline B06 (T-GCN) records the worst demand MAE at 257.21 MW and macro R² of −0.483. Linear regression B01 remains far above the competitive tier at 247.79 MW MAE and MAPE 17.32%. The pattern shows that graph conditioning alone, in the form of a shallow spatio-temporal GCN \cite{Yu2018STGCN}, does not translate into accurate regional forecasts on this dataset, whereas the PF-STGT family—whether hybrid (B07) or correlation-only (S2)—consistently outperforms both classical ensembles and standalone recurrent encoders.

A notable secondary observation concerns demand R². Random forest B02 reports macro R² of 0.984, exceeding S2 (0.684) and B07 (0.674). R² and MAE quantify different aspects of predictive performance—variance explained relative to the regional mean versus mean absolute megawatt deviation—and macro demand MAE remains the predefined primary ranking metric in Table 3. On that criterion, S2 and B07 rank ahead of B02 despite the higher classical R².

S2 improves upon B07 by 4.66 MW in mean demand MAE (88.65 versus 93.31 MW), a reduction of approximately 5.0% relative to the historical reference. Within the multi-task models reported in Table 3, S2 thus represents the stronger demand forecaster. In absolute terms, reductions of this magnitude—and the 8.38 MW point-estimate gap between S2 and random forest B02—represent measurable decreases in mean absolute megawatt forecast error on the test split, without extending the benchmark evidence to quantified downstream operational or economic effects. The practical implication is that adopting the correlation-only graph variant of PF-STGT yields modest but consistent megawatt-level gains over the earlier hybrid-graph configuration without relinquishing joint stress prediction.

### 8.1.3 Operational Stress prediction

Task 2 performance is reported only for models that produce graph-level OSI forecasts. In Table 3, S2 achieves stress MAE of 0.0371 and stress R² of 0.745—the highest OSI explanatory power among all benchmarked models. B07 records stress MAE of 0.0499 and stress R² of 0.585. Classical and recurrent baselines that supply auxiliary stress outputs perform worse: B02 stress R² is 0.555 (MAE 0.0481), B03 is 0.525 (MAE 0.0497), and B04–B06 yield negative stress R² values ranging from −0.191 to −0.304 with stress MAE between 0.086 and 0.089.

The proposed model therefore delivers both the lowest OSI absolute error and the strongest variance explanation on the test split. Relative to B07, S2 reduces stress MAE from 0.0499 to 0.0371 and raises stress R² by 0.160 absolute (0.585 to 0.745). For grid operators monitoring operational pressure alongside load, this indicates that the final multi-task configuration not only improves demand forecasts but also provides a substantially more informative day-ahead stress signal than the historical PF-STGT reference and than any stress-capable baseline in the benchmark suite.

### 8.1.4 Statistical evidence

Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests \cite{Wilcoxon1945SignedRank} on per-window macro demand MAE (n = 264) provide inferential support for the benchmark rankings in **Table 4**. With B07 as the reference and Bonferroni-adjusted significance threshold \cite{Dunn1961Bonferroni} α = 0.0083 across six comparisons, B07 achieves statistically significant lower demand MAE than every baseline B01–B06. Median paired improvements range from 4.92 MW against random forest B02 to 160.66 MW against T-GCN B06. Bootstrap 95% confidence intervals \cite{Efron1993Bootstrap} on mean paired MAE differences exclude zero for comparisons against B01, B03, B04, B05, and B06; the interval for B07 versus B02 is [−8.87, 2.62] MW, which spans zero despite a significant Wilcoxon result (p = 0.00135, Cohen's d \cite{Cohen1988StatisticalPower} = −0.077).

Direct inferential evidence for S2 versus the B07 reference is available from the paired Wilcoxon comparison on the same test windows: median ΔMAE = −5.43 MW, two-sided p = 5.5×10⁻⁵, bootstrap 95% CI [−7.17, −2.16] MW. This interval excludes zero and corroborates the point estimate improvement seen in Table 3. No formal paired Wilcoxon test of S2 against random forest B02 was conducted in the benchmark experiment; the point estimate advantage of 8.38 MW (97.03 − 88.65) should be interpreted alongside the significant B07-versus-B02 result rather than as an independently tested claim.

Taken together, the statistical record supports systematic PF-STGT superiority over classical, recurrent, and GCN baselines on megawatt MAE, confirms a significant incremental gain of the final S2 configuration over the B07 reference, and cautions that the B07–versus–random forest improvement—while statistically significant—carries a small standardized effect size and a bootstrap interval compatible with near-zero mean improvement. Dispatch applications should weight megawatt point estimates and confidence bounds alongside p-values when judging operational relevance.

### 8.1.5 Overall interpretation

The frozen benchmark results establish S2 as the leading multi-task configuration in Table 3 on both reported tasks: lowest macro demand MAE (88.65 MW) and highest stress R² (0.745) on 264 held-out test windows. The PF-STGT family consistently outperforms the standalone LSTM, GRU, and T-GCN architectures, whose demand errors exceed 230 MW and whose stress predictions fail to outperform the test-set mean. Classical ensembles remain competitive tabular benchmarks—random forest in particular—but do not match the megawatt accuracy of the proposed graph-transformer model despite higher reported macro R².

The principal strength of the benchmark outcome is the joint delivery of accurate regional demand forecasts and informative OSI predictions from a single multi-task model, with statistically supported improvements over both external baselines and the prior hybrid-graph PF-STGT reference. The principal limitation visible at this stage is that demand MAE gains over the strongest non–PF-STGT baseline (random forest) are modest in absolute terms and accompanied by a wide bootstrap confidence interval in the B07-versus–B02 test, and that classical models can exhibit favourable R² without corresponding MAE leadership. These results validate the proposed framework as the preferred multi-task forecasting configuration in Table 3 while indicating that absolute megawatt improvements over well-tuned ensemble methods, though consistent, should be communicated with appropriate statistical qualification rather than as unqualified dominance across every secondary metric.

## 8.2 Regional Forecast Analysis

### 8.2.1 Macro context and regional scope

Section 8.1 established model rankings using macro-averaged demand MAE over nine administrative divisions on 264 test windows. **Figure 4** visualises that macro comparison, with S2 attaining the lowest bar at 88.65 MW ahead of B07 (93.31 MW), B02 (97.03 MW), B03 (109.73 MW), and B06 (257.21 MW) as reported in Table 3. Because macro MAE assigns equal weight to each division, the chart summarises national leaderboard performance but does not reveal how forecast error is distributed across regions with markedly different load scales.

Division-level demand MAE for S2 is reported in the supplementary ablation outputs; per-region MAE and R² for B07 and B02 appear in Table S1. Together with Figure 4 and Table 3, these sources permit a disaggregated reading of the macro results without altering the rankings established in Section 8.1. For operators, the practical value of this regional view is to identify where absolute megawatt errors concentrate and whether macro gains reflect uniform improvement or selective division-level shifts.

### 8.2.2 Regional demand performance

Under S2, per-region test MAE ranges from 32.73 MW (Barishal) to 293.98 MW (Dhaka), with the full nine-division profile tabulated in the supplementary ablation outputs. The three lowest-MAE divisions—Barishal, Sylhet, and Rangpur—each remain below 55 MW, whereas Dhaka alone exceeds 290 MW and Khulna exceeds 88 MW. At the macro level, S2 records demand RMSE of 127.29 MW, demand MAPE of 6.55%, and macro demand R² of 0.684 on the test split.

Relative to B07 (Table S1), peripheral accuracy is broadly comparable but not uniform: S2 records slightly higher MAE in Barishal, Chattogram, Cumilla, Mymensingh, and Sylhet, while reducing MAE in Khulna (88.62 versus 118.72 MW), Rajshahi (76.25 versus 85.70 MW), Rangpur (53.97 versus 59.77 MW), and Dhaka (293.98 versus 299.78 MW). Against random forest B02, S2 achieves lower Dhaka and Khulna MAE while trailing B02 in Barishal and several other divisions by small margins. The interpretation is that S2’s macro leadership in Section 8.1 arises from a mixture of division-level gains and minor regressions rather than from uniform error reduction everywhere. Planners should therefore treat the macro bar in Figure 4 as an equal-weight summary while consulting Table S1 when prioritising transmission corridors or load centres with the largest absolute residuals.

### 8.2.3 Regional consistency and variability

Per-region MAE under S2 exhibits substantial cross-division spread: the ratio between Dhaka MAE (293.98 MW) and Barishal MAE (32.73 MW) exceeds ninefold on the test split. B07 and B02 display a similar pattern in Table S1, with Dhaka MAE of 299.78 MW and 311.59 MW respectively against peripheral MAE values generally below 90 MW. This consistency of error concentration across model families indicates that regional heterogeneity in absolute megawatt error is a persistent feature of the test period rather than an artefact of a single architecture.

Comparing S2 with B07 division by division, the largest improvements occur in Khulna (−30.10 MW), Rajshahi (−9.45 MW), Dhaka (−5.80 MW), and Rangpur (−5.80 MW), whereas S2 records slightly higher MAE than B07 in five peripheral divisions, each by less than 5 MW in absolute terms. The underlying cause of this asymmetric pattern is not established by the benchmark outputs alone and would require further investigation beyond the scope of the present results. The practical implication is that the macro MAE gain of 4.66 MW (S2 versus B07) aggregates offsetting regional effects; deployment reviews should examine division-level metrics alongside the national summary in Figure 4.

Per-region R² values in Table S1 further illustrate metric divergence. For B07, division-level R² ranges from 0.4808 (Barishal) to 0.8270 (Rajshahi); for B02, Rajshahi reaches 0.8274 while Dhaka records 0.6666 despite B02’s higher Dhaka MAE than S2. R² and MAE therefore need not agree at the division level, consistent with the macro-level distinction noted in Section 8.1.2. Forecast quality assessments that rely on a single metric may reach different conclusions depending on whether absolute megawatt deviation or variance explanation is emphasised.

### 8.2.4 Dhaka-specific performance

Dhaka carries the largest evening-peak load among the nine divisions and accounts for approximately 35.7% of mean national regional demand in the exploratory data analysis. On the test split, Dhaka also exhibits the highest demand variability among audited regions, with B07 recording an observed demand standard deviation of 670.15 MW in Table S1—far exceeding peripheral divisions. Dhaka MAE under S2 is 293.98 MW, compared with 299.78 MW for B07 and 311.59 MW for B02 (Table S1); the supplementary audit reports Dhaka R² of 0.5744 for B07 and 0.6666 for B02 on the same split.

S2 therefore achieves the lowest audited Dhaka MAE among the three models with published division-level tables, reducing absolute Dhaka error by 5.80 MW relative to B07 and 17.61 MW relative to B02. Because Dhaka’s load scale dominates national totals, even modest percentage improvements translate into larger absolute megawatt reductions than comparable percentage gains in low-load divisions. Nevertheless, Dhaka MAE remains an order of magnitude above peripheral MAE under S2, so the principal national load centre continues to account for the largest share of forecast error in megawatt terms. Day-ahead planning for the capital region should continue to treat Dhaka as a separately reported diagnostic rather than relying solely on macro-averaged scores.

### 8.2.5 Regional strengths and limitations

The strongest regional performance under S2 occurs in Barishal, Sylhet, and Rangpur, where MAE remains below 55 MW, and in Mymensingh (59.22 MW). These divisions contribute relatively little to the macro numerator despite receiving equal weight in the macro average. In the present benchmark, S2 also delivers its largest MAE reductions relative to B07 in Khulna and Rajshahi, indicating that the proposed model improves selected mid-scale divisions with material evening-peak load without registering worse MAE in every peripheral node.

The principal regional limitation is the persistent concentration of absolute error in Dhaka, where S2 MAE (293.98 MW) still far exceeds all other divisions. Secondary limitations include small MAE regressions versus B07 in five divisions—Barishal, Chattogram, Cumilla, Mymensingh, and Sylhet—each below 5 MW in absolute terms, and the absence of a published per-region R² table for S2 in the supplementary materials, which restricts division-level variance diagnostics for the proposed model to MAE and the macro R² summary (0.684). Regional patterns beyond the reported MAE and R² values—such as seasonal or event-driven error clustering—are not characterised in the benchmark artefacts and are deferred to dedicated error-analysis reporting. Overall, the regional evidence supports S2 as a consistent multi-division forecaster with operationally meaningful Dhaka gains and stable peripheral accuracy, while highlighting that equal-weight macro summaries in Figure 4 can understate the national planning significance of the largest load centre.

## 8.3 Operational Stress (OSI) Analysis

### 8.3.1 Scope and evaluation context

Section 8.1 introduced joint demand and OSI rankings from Table 3; this subsection examines Task 2 in greater depth. Operational Stress Index prediction is evaluated on the same held-out test split of 264 forecast windows used for demand benchmarking, with graph-level OSI reported as mean absolute error (MAE) and coefficient of determination (R²) on the scalar stress target in \([0, 1]\). Only models that emit an OSI forecast appear in the stress columns of Table 3; the demand-only configuration A4 in Table 5 is excluded from OSI comparison because it does not produce a stress output.

The frozen evaluation record therefore permits two complementary readings: an external benchmark comparison of S2 against stress-capable baselines B01–B07 (Table 3), and an internal configuration comparison across the multi-task variants tabulated in Table 5. **Figure 5** visualises test-set demand MAE for the frozen variants in Table 5 and is referenced here alongside Table 5’s stress columns to characterise how demand and stress accuracy co-vary across configurations. For operators who require a day-ahead stress signal alongside regional load forecasts, the relevant question is whether the final proposed model delivers the strongest joint stress evidence within the frozen suite rather than optimising either task in isolation.

### 8.3.2 OSI benchmark comparison

Among all stress-capable entries in **Table 3**, S2 attains the lowest OSI MAE at 0.0371 and the highest OSI R² at 0.745 on the 264-window test split. The historical PF-STGT reference B07 records OSI MAE of 0.0499 and OSI R² of 0.585. Classical ensemble baselines that supply auxiliary stress outputs remain above S2 on both metrics: random forest B02 achieves OSI MAE of 0.0481 with R² of 0.555, and gradient boosting B03 records OSI MAE of 0.0497 with R² of 0.525. Recurrent and graph-convolution baselines B04–B06 yield OSI MAE between 0.0861 and 0.0891 with negative stress R² values ranging from −0.191 to −0.304, indicating that their stress predictions fail to outperform the test-set mean. Linear regression B01 records the largest OSI MAE at 0.1074 and stress R² of −1.824.

Relative to B07, S2 reduces OSI MAE by 0.0128 absolute (0.0499 to 0.0371, approximately 25.7%) and raises stress R² by 0.160 absolute (0.585 to 0.745). Relative to the strongest classical stress competitor in Table 3, random forest B02, S2 improves OSI MAE by 0.0110 (0.0481 to 0.0371) and stress R² by 0.190 (0.555 to 0.745). The benchmark pattern shows that the proposed multi-task graph-transformer configuration occupies the leading position on both reported OSI metrics, while shallow recurrent and T-GCN architectures do not yield informative day-ahead stress forecasts on this split. Grid operators comparing stress-capable models should treat S2 as the preferred OSI forecaster within the frozen external benchmark, with the caveat that classical baselines remain closer on absolute OSI MAE than on variance explanation.

### 8.3.3 Configuration-level OSI performance

**Table 5** reports OSI metrics for the frozen multi-task configuration suite evaluated on the identical test split. S2 (variant A6) records OSI MAE of 0.0371 and OSI R² of 0.745. The historical hybrid-graph reference A1 (equivalent to B07) records OSI MAE of 0.0499 and OSI R² of 0.585. Variants A2 (no graph) and A3 (no transformer) each achieve OSI MAE of 0.0405 and OSI R² of 0.701—lower absolute error and higher R² than A1 but still behind A6 on both stress metrics. Variant A5 (geographical graph only) attains the lowest OSI MAE among multi-task configurations at 0.0340 and the highest stress R² at 0.764, while recording demand MAE of 97.98 MW. Variant A4 (single-task) achieves the lowest demand MAE in the suite at 86.89 MW but provides no OSI output.

**Figure 5** orders the same configurations by test demand MAE: A4 at 86.89 MW, A6 (S2) at 88.65 MW, A3 at 92.64 MW, A1 at 93.31 MW, A2 at 93.93 MW, and A5 at 97.98 MW. Read together with Table 5, the figure and table show that lower demand MAE does not imply superior OSI accuracy across configurations. A5 leads on both OSI metrics yet records the highest demand MAE among the multi-task variants shown in Figure 5, whereas A6 combines the second-lowest demand bar in that chart with the second-best OSI MAE and R² in Table 5. The benchmark artefacts demonstrate this demand–stress divergence but do not establish the underlying mechanism responsible for variant-level trade-offs. For joint day-ahead planning, S2 represents the configuration that simultaneously delivers the lowest multi-task demand MAE among OSI-producing variants in Figure 5 and the strongest OSI R² in the external benchmark of Table 3, accepting that A5 attains marginally lower OSI MAE and higher stress R² at the cost of 9.33 MW higher demand error relative to S2.

### 8.3.4 Statistical evidence and reporting limits

Formal inferential testing in the frozen statistical record is defined on per-window macro **demand** MAE rather than on OSI residuals. The paired Wilcoxon comparison of A1 versus A6 (S2) reports median demand ΔMAE of −5.43 MW, two-sided p = 5.5×10⁻⁵, and bootstrap 95% confidence interval [−7.17, −2.16] MW on the test split (Table 5; statistical summary). That interval excludes zero and corroborates the demand improvement of S2 over the historical reference; no corresponding Wilcoxon test, p-value, or bootstrap confidence interval for OSI MAE or OSI R² was computed in Experiments 02 or 03.

Stress improvement from A1 to A6 is therefore supported only by point estimates: OSI MAE decreases from 0.0499 to 0.0371 and OSI R² increases from 0.585 to 0.745. Benchmark comparisons of S2 against classical baselines on OSI similarly rest on Table 3 point estimates without paired stress hypothesis tests. Wilcoxon results for B07 versus B01–B06 in Table 4 establish significant demand MAE separation for the PF-STGT reference but do not extend to the stress task. The interpretation is that OSI performance claims in this section should be communicated as consistent point-estimate advantages on the frozen test split, while demand-side inferential evidence provides indirect support that the final configuration improves upon the historical reference on the primary optimisation objective without substituting for stress-specific significance testing.

### 8.3.5 Practical implications and limitations

The frozen OSI evidence indicates that S2 delivers graph-level stress forecasts with MAE below 0.04 and R² of 0.745, explaining approximately three-quarters of test-set OSI variance while also leading the demand benchmark in Section 8.1. Operators integrating a single multi-task model for next-day regional load and national stress monitoring can rely on S2 as the configuration with the strongest joint benchmark credentials in Tables 3 and 5, rather than deploying a classical baseline that trails on stress R² (B02: 0.555; B03: 0.525) or a recurrent architecture whose stress predictions are uninformative relative to the mean.

Three limitations follow directly from the reported artefacts. First, no stress-specific confidence intervals or multiple-comparison-corrected tests are available; OSI gains over B07 and classical baselines should be qualified accordingly. Second, configuration A5 achieves lower OSI MAE (0.0340) and higher stress R² (0.764) than S2 but at materially higher demand MAE (97.98 versus 88.65 MW), so stress-optimal and demand-optimal configurations diverge within the frozen suite. Third, variant A4 establishes a demand-only performance bound of 86.89 MW—1.76 MW below S2—without furnishing any OSI forecast, confirming an explicit demand–stress Pareto trade-off rather than simultaneous optimality on both tasks. The benchmark demonstrates these patterns on the 2024 test period but does not quantify downstream operational or economic consequences of OSI forecast error; deployment decisions should treat the reported MAE and R² values as accuracy diagnostics to be monitored alongside division-level demand metrics from Section 8.2.

## 8.4 Ablation Study

### 8.4.1 Ablation scope and reference configuration

The frozen ablation programme evaluates six PF-STGT configurations (A1–A6) on the held-out test split of 264 forecast windows, with A1 serving as the reference: hybrid geographical–correlation graph, full transformer trunk, and multi-task demand–OSI learning under the W20 protocol. **Table 5** consolidates test-set macro demand MAE, demand R², stress MAE, and stress R² for each variant; **Figure 5** visualises the same configurations ranked by demand MAE. Together, the table and figure permit component-wise comparison against A1 without rerunning experiments.

A1 (equivalent to benchmark reference B07) records demand MAE of 93.31 MW, demand R² of 0.674, stress MAE of 0.0499, and stress R² of 0.585. All subsequent ablation contrasts in this section are interpreted relative to that reference. The design implication is that any architectural change should be judged against this frozen multi-task baseline rather than against external baselines alone, because A1 encodes the original hybrid-graph PF-STGT configuration from which S2 was derived.

### 8.4.2 Graph contribution

Graph topology is isolated by three variants: A2 removes graph conditioning entirely while retaining the hybrid multi-task trunk; A5 replaces the hybrid adjacency with a geographical graph only; and A6 (S2) replaces it with a correlation-only graph (τ = 0.65). On demand MAE, A2 records 93.93 MW—0.62 MW above A1—whereas A5 records 97.98 MW, the highest demand error among the six configurations. A6 achieves 88.65 MW, reducing demand MAE by 4.66 MW relative to A1 (approximately 5.0%). Paired Wilcoxon testing reports median demand ΔMAE of +2.81 MW for A1 versus A2 (p = 0.301, bootstrap 95% CI [−3.69, 4.93] MW), +3.85 MW for A1 versus A5 (p = 1.48×10⁻⁴, Bonferroni significant at α = 0.01, CI [2.19, 6.90] MW), and −5.43 MW for A1 versus A6 (p = 5.5×10⁻⁵, CI [−7.17, −2.16] MW).

On the stress task, A2 improves upon A1 (stress MAE 0.0405 versus 0.0499; stress R² 0.701 versus 0.585), whereas A5 attains the lowest stress MAE in Table 5 at 0.0340 with stress R² of 0.764—marginally above A6 (0.0371 MAE, 0.745 R²). The ablation evidence shows that graph choice materially affects both tasks, but not uniformly: removing the graph module on the hybrid trunk does not yield a statistically significant demand change, geographical-only adjacency significantly degrades demand, and correlation-only adjacency significantly improves demand while also raising stress R² relative to A1. The design implication is that, among the graph variants evaluated in the present study (A2, A5, and A6), correlation-graph conditioning is the only variant that simultaneously delivers statistically supported demand improvement over A1; geographical-only priors should not be retained when demand accuracy is prioritised, and the hybrid graph is not demand-optimal relative to A6 despite its role as the historical reference.

### 8.4.3 Transformer contribution

Variant A3 removes the transformer encoder while preserving the hybrid graph and multi-task heads. A3 records demand MAE of 92.64 MW—0.67 MW below A1—and stress MAE of 0.0405 with stress R² of 0.701, compared with A1 values of 93.31 MW, 0.0499, and 0.585 respectively. Wilcoxon comparison of A1 versus A3 yields median demand ΔMAE of −1.13 MW, two-sided p = 0.384, and bootstrap 95% CI [−2.35, 1.08] MW, which spans zero; the comparison is not significant at the Bonferroni-adjusted threshold α = 0.01.

Within the A1–A6 ablation grid, transformer removal on the hybrid topology therefore produces numerically lower demand MAE and improved stress metrics relative to A1, but the demand difference is small and not statistically distinguishable from zero on the test split. The ablation record alone does not establish whether the transformer is redundant on all graph topologies; it demonstrates that, for the hybrid graph reference, A3 and A1 perform similarly on demand MAE. The design implication is that the current ablation does not provide sufficient evidence to recommend transformer removal on hybrid-graph grounds, because the non-significant demand gap does not justify sacrificing the full encoder without further topology-specific validation.

### 8.4.4 Multi-task contribution

Multi-task learning is isolated by variant A4, which disables the OSI head and trains demand only on the hybrid graph. A4 achieves the lowest demand MAE in the ablation suite at 86.89 MW with demand R² of 0.731, compared with A1 at 93.31 MW and 0.674. Wilcoxon comparison of A1 versus A4 reports median demand ΔMAE of −5.25 MW, p = 0.00284, and bootstrap 95% CI [−10.63, −2.40] MW; A4 improves demand relative to the multi-task reference A1, though the Bonferroni flag for “significantly worse” does not apply because A4 is better, not worse. A4 provides no stress MAE or stress R² entries in Table 5.

Relative to the final multi-task configuration A6 (S2), A4 retains a 1.76 MW demand advantage (86.89 versus 88.65 MW) but forfeits OSI forecasting entirely. Conversely, A6 improves demand by 4.66 MW over A1 while delivering stress R² of 0.745—0.160 above A1—indicating that multi-task training under the correlation graph does not reproduce the demand-only ceiling but does enable joint stress prediction at substantially higher quality than A1. The design implication is that multi-task learning trades approximately 1.76 MW of demand MAE relative to the single-task upper bound in exchange for OSI outputs with R² above 0.74; operators requiring only regional demand may prefer A4’s megawatt accuracy, whereas joint load-and-stress planning necessitates a multi-task variant such as A6.

### 8.4.5 Demand–stress trade-offs across A1–A6

**Figure 5** orders all six configurations by demand MAE: A4 (86.89 MW), A6 (88.65 MW), A3 (92.64 MW), A1 (93.31 MW), A2 (93.93 MW), and A5 (97.98 MW). Read with Table 5, the ranking shows that the lowest demand bar (A4) corresponds to the absence of stress outputs, while the second-lowest (A6) pairs the best multi-task demand score with stress R² of 0.745. A5 occupies the highest demand position yet attains the best stress MAE (0.0340) and stress R² (0.764) among OSI-producing variants, confirming that stress-optimal and demand-optimal configurations diverge within the frozen suite. A2 and A3 cluster near A1 on demand while recording identical stress MAE (0.0405) and stress R² (0.701), suggesting similar joint-task behaviour when either the graph module or transformer is removed on the hybrid trunk.

The benchmark artefacts document these trade-offs as point estimates on 264 test windows; they do not establish causal mechanisms for why A5 excels on stress while lagging on demand. The design implication is that no single ablation row simultaneously minimises demand MAE and maximises both stress metrics: selecting a production configuration requires an explicit priority over joint versus demand-only operation and over demand versus stress emphasis within multi-task candidates.

### 8.4.6 Final model selection

Architecture A6 (correlation-only graph, full transformer, multi-task) is designated S2—the final proposed model—based on its position in Table 5 and Figure 5. A6 delivers the lowest demand MAE among all multi-task configurations (88.65 MW), with statistically supported improvement over A1 (median ΔMAE −5.43 MW, p = 5.5×10⁻⁵, bootstrap 95% CI [−7.17, −2.16] MW). It simultaneously records stress R² of 0.745 and stress MAE of 0.0371, exceeding A1 on both stress metrics and matching the S2 row in the external benchmark of Table 3. A6 is not demand-optimal within the ablation suite—A4 remains 1.76 MW lower—and is not stress-optimal among multi-task variants—A5 records lower stress MAE and higher stress R² at the cost of 9.33 MW additional demand error.

Selection of A6 as the final configuration therefore reflects a joint-task criterion: best multi-task demand accuracy with strong OSI performance and statistically significant improvement over the historical hybrid reference, rather than optimisation of either task in isolation. The design implication for the frozen architecture is adoption of correlation-only graph conditioning with the full transformer trunk and multi-task W20 heads as S2, reflecting the documented demand–stress Pareto trade-off relative to A4 and A5 while meeting the joint forecasting requirement established in Sections 8.1 and 8.3.

## 8.5 Error Analysis

### 8.5.1 Scope and aggregate error magnitudes

Sections 8.1–8.4 established benchmark and configuration rankings for S2; this subsection characterises the magnitude and structure of remaining forecast errors on the held-out test split rather than restating model superiority. The frozen evaluation record for S2 (variant A6) reports macro demand MAE of 88.65 MW, demand RMSE of 127.29 MW, demand MAPE of 6.55%, and macro demand R² of 0.684, together with OSI MAE of 0.0371, OSI RMSE of 0.0473, and OSI R² of 0.745 on 264 test windows (Table 3; `ablation_raw.json`).

The ratio of demand RMSE to demand MAE is approximately 1.44 (127.29/88.65), indicating that squared-error emphasis falls on a subset of windows or regions with larger absolute deviations than the macro mean. For OSI, the RMSE-to-MAE ratio is approximately 1.27 (0.0473/0.0371), suggesting comparatively less tail amplification relative to demand on the normalised stress scale. Pooled signed-residual statistics—mean bias, skewness, and histogram shape—for S2 are not tabulated in the frozen results package; the planned error-analysis framework defined post-hoc residual protocols, but residual-store generation was not executed for the final checkpoint. Aggregate error magnitudes therefore rest on MAE, RMSE, and R² summaries rather than on full distributional diagnostics for S2.

### 8.5.2 Regional error concentration

Per-region demand MAE for S2 on the test split ranges from 32.73 MW (Barishal) to 293.98 MW (Dhaka), with Khulna at 88.62 MW, Rajshahi at 76.25 MW, Rangpur at 53.97 MW, Sylhet at 40.62 MW, Mymensingh at 59.22 MW, Chattogram at 76.52 MW, and Cumilla at 75.92 MW (`ablation_raw.json`, variant A6). Dhaka MAE exceeds the next-largest division (Khulna) by more than 205 MW and accounts for the dominant share of the macro numerator despite equal per-division weighting in the macro average.

The same concentration pattern appears for the historical hybrid reference B07 in Table S1 (Dhaka MAE 299.78 MW versus peripheral values generally below 90 MW), indicating that high absolute error in the capital division is a persistent feature of the test period across PF-STGT configurations rather than an artefact isolated to S2. S2 reduces Dhaka MAE by 5.80 MW relative to B07 (293.98 versus 299.78 MW) while retaining a ninefold gap between Dhaka and Barishal MAE. The interpretation is that remaining demand error is structurally concentrated in the largest load centre; the limitation is that per-region signed residuals and box-plot distributions for S2 are not published in the frozen supplementary materials, restricting regional diagnostics to absolute MAE.

### 8.5.3 Demand versus stress error comparison

On the common test split, S2 explains approximately 68.4% of macro demand variance (R² = 0.684) while explaining 74.5% of OSI variance (R² = 0.745). Absolute demand error is reported in megawatts (macro MAE 88.65 MW), whereas OSI error is reported on the unit interval (MAE 0.0371), so the two tasks are not directly comparable in physical units; the relevant comparison is relative explanatory power and absolute error on each task’s native scale.

Demand MAPE of 6.55% indicates that typical regional percentage deviations remain modest at the macro level, but megawatt RMSE of 127.29 MW shows that demand errors on high-load days or in high-load divisions can exceed the mean absolute level. OSI MAE below 0.04 with R² of 0.745 indicates that graph-level stress forecasts track the held-out OSI series more closely in variance terms than demand forecasts do, despite demand being the primary optimisation weight in training. Representative test-day case records from Experiment 04 illustrate isolated stress deviations: on 2024-09-08 (high-stress stratum), observed OSI is 0.565 while the S2 prediction is 0.429, whereas on 2024-10-27 (typical-demand stratum) observed OSI is 0.426 and the prediction is 0.411 (`case_studies.md`). The benchmark demonstrates that large single-day OSI gaps can occur on the test split, but four published test case studies are insufficient to characterise systematic temporal or regime-dependent stress bias; that mechanism remains unresolved in the frozen artefacts.

### 8.5.4 Coalition-level error structure

**Figure 6a** and **Figure 6b** present frozen coalition-level summary plots from Experiment 04 for the S2 checkpoint, visualising how forecast deviations associate with grouped input coalitions on the stress task (Figure 6a) and on Dhaka demand (Figure 6b). On the stress task, limitation stack (G8) and calendar/trend (G6) coalitions show the widest spread among the plotted groups; grid aggregates (G7) and national generation scalars (G10) exhibit narrower spread (Figure 6a; frozen Exp04 coalition rankings). On Dhaka demand, calendar/trend (G6), engineered lags and rolling statistics (G4), and national generation scalars (G10) show the widest spread, with regional demand and supply blocks (G1, G2) at intermediate levels (Figure 6b).

The plots indicate that remaining forecast error co-occurs with variability in calendar, limitation, and lagged-demand input coalitions rather than being uniformly distributed across all feature groups. The repository evidence does not establish whether errors are driven by mis-specification of those coalitions or by unmodelled events outside the input tensor; causal attribution is not resolved by the error summaries alone. The limitation is that these coalition plots summarise association structure on the frozen evaluation sample and do not replace per-window residual traces or signed bias tests for S2.

### 8.5.5 Temporal behaviour and residual diagnostics

The predefined error-analysis protocol specified temporal, extreme-event, and graph-conditioned error segments, but no executed temporal MAE bins, monthly residual profiles, or pooled residual store for S2 appear in the frozen results package. Temporal error behaviour on the 2024 test period therefore cannot be reported as a segmented time series from repository outputs.

Experiment 04 includes four representative test dates (2024-10-27, 2024-04-28, 2024-12-19, 2024-09-08) spanning typical-demand, high-demand, low-demand, and high-stress strata (`case_studies.md`). These records show day-specific demand totals between 8,866 MW and 16,418 MW and OSI values between 0.344 and 0.565 on the test split, confirming that the held-out year contains diverse operating points, but they do not constitute a systematic temporal error audit. Equivalent pooled signed-residual statistics are not available for S2 in the frozen evaluation record; the historical PF-STGT reference B07 is therefore discussed below for contextual orientation only. Experiment 02A reports pooled residual diagnostics for B07—mean residual −19.58 MW, median −18.97 MW, residual skew −2.998, and pred-to-actual standard-deviation ratio 0.983 with attenuated peak tracking (`residual_analysis.md`, `prediction_distribution_analysis.md`). Those values characterise the prior hybrid-graph configuration and cannot be transferred to S2 without a dedicated residual audit of the final checkpoint. The limitation is that temporal and signed-residual characterisation of S2 remains incomplete in the present evaluation.

### 8.5.6 Deployment implications

The frozen error evidence supports deploying S2 as a joint forecaster with macro demand MAE of 88.65 MW and OSI R² of 0.745, while treating Dhaka as a separately monitored division: its MAE of 293.98 MW dominates national error budgets despite S2 achieving the lowest audited Dhaka MAE among models with published division tables (Section 8.2). Operators should expect comparatively well-calibrated graph-level stress forecasts on average, with awareness that high-stress test days documented in Experiment 04 can exhibit OSI under-prediction exceeding the macro stress MAE.

Coalition-level summaries in Figures 6a and 6b suggest that deployment monitoring should include limitation-stack and calendar inputs alongside lagged demand channels, because forecast deviations cluster with variability in those coalitions on the frozen evaluation. Without executed temporal segmentation under the planned error-analysis framework or S2 signed-residual histograms, routine operational guardrails should rely on published macro and per-region MAE/R² thresholds, division-level Dhaka reporting, and periodic stress-case review rather than on repository-supplied uncertainty intervals or automated bias alarms. A limitation of the current evaluation is the absence of complete S2 residual time-series diagnostics and uncertainty bounds; remaining error structure is therefore documented through aggregate metrics, regional concentration, and coalition associations rather than through signed residual traces or forecast confidence intervals on the test split.

## 8.6 Explainability Analysis

### 8.6.1 Scope and attribution methods

The explainability evaluation applies post-hoc attribution methods \cite{Lundberg2017SHAP,Sundararajan2017IntegratedGradients} to the frozen S2 checkpoint (variant A6, seed 42) without retraining or altering the benchmark metrics reported in Sections 8.1–8.5. **Table 7** consolidates headline outputs from Experiment 04: grouped GradientSHAP attributions (reported as coalition magnitudes |φ|), coalition-level permutation importance, exported spatial and temporal attention weights, and 24 stratified case studies (20 validation, 4 test). Coalition |φ| values are obtained through a Captum-compatible integrated-gradients loop using a GradientSHAP approximation with 25 steps and a zero baseline—a SHAP-style attribution pipeline rather than classical exact Shapley enumeration (`shap_summary.md`; `shap_engine.py`).

The frozen record does not include a separate standalone Captum attribution export distinct from this integrated-gradient pipeline; cross-method checks therefore compare gradient-based coalition attributions against permutation importance and OSI component decomposition rather than against an independent Captum ranking. The practical implication is that coalition importance below should be read as model attribution on the frozen validation sample, not as proof of physical causation or operator-ground-truth driver validation.

### 8.6.2 Coalition-level gradient attribution

On the global stress task, grouped GradientSHAP magnitudes rank limitation stack (G8) first at |φ| = 0.0191 and calendar/trend (G6) second at |φ| = 0.0190, followed by grid aggregates (G7) at 0.0087 and national generation scalars (G10) at 0.0082 (Table 7; `shap_summary.md`, validation n = 20). For Dhaka demand, calendar/trend (G6) records |φ| = 162.34, engineered lags and rolling statistics (G4) record 101.26, and national generation scalars (G10) record 91.44; regional demand and supply blocks (G1, G2) follow at 77.72 and 77.41 respectively, with limitation stack (G8) far lower at 23.15 on this task.

The stress and demand heads therefore emphasise different coalition families: limitation and calendar channels dominate stress attributions, whereas Dhaka demand attributions concentrate on calendar structure, autoregressive lag features, and national generation context. The GradientSHAP pipeline recovers this task-specific separation on the frozen sample. Operators reviewing day-ahead outputs should treat limitation-stack and calendar coalitions as the primary stress attribution channels and calendar/lag coalitions as the primary Dhaka demand channels, while recognising that attribution magnitude does not establish which inputs physically cause forecast changes.

### 8.6.3 Spatial node attribution

**Figure 7** presents the node-level importance heatmap combining mean GradientSHAP node mass and spatial attention on the correlation graph. Dhaka receives the highest mean node attribution mass at 340.36, followed by Rajshahi (110.32), Khulna (108.91), and Mymensingh (93.84); peripheral nodes record substantially lower mass, with Rangpur at 1.99 and Chattogram at 6.21 (Table 7; `node_attribution.md`). The Spearman correlation between learned spatial attention and the frozen correlation adjacency is ρ = 0.422.

The spatial pattern shows that attribution mass concentrates on divisions with large evening-peak load and strong graph connectivity to Dhaka, consistent with the regional error concentration documented in Section 8.5. Moderate attention–adjacency alignment indicates partial—but not complete—correspondence between learned message passing and the train-estimated correlation prior. The practical implication is that spatial explanations support monitoring Dhaka and its high-mass neighbours jointly, while ρ = 0.422 cautions against equating attention patterns with the full correlation graph structure.

### 8.6.4 Temporal attribution

**Figure 8** plots mean temporal attention weights α_t across the seven-day input window. Weights are near-uniform, with the largest mean weight at lag t−6 (α = 0.1622), followed by t−3 (0.1459) and t−0 (0.1435); remaining lags range from 0.1348 to 0.1405 (`temporal_attribution.md`). Case-study records most frequently list t−6 among the top three temporal lags (Table 7).

The near-uniform profile indicates limited temporal selectivity within the lookback window on the frozen evaluation sample: no single day dominates attention across cases. The model therefore distributes temporal credit broadly rather than concentrating on one lag, an observation that is consistent with the non-significant demand effect of transformer removal on the hybrid reference in Section 8.4, although the present attribution analysis alone cannot establish that relationship. For deployment, temporal attributions suggest that forecasts respond to the full week of context rather than to a single recent day, but they do not identify which calendar periods drive remaining forecast error.

### 8.6.5 OSI dual-path stress attribution

**Figure 9** compares grouped GradientSHAP stress attributions with OSI component drivers (reserve margin \(c_2\) versus limitation stack \(c_3\)) across the 24 case studies. The dominant stress coalition in case records is G8 (limitation_stack); peak-demand strata sometimes rank G7 (grid_aggregates) highest. Dual-path agreement between the top gradient-attribution coalition and the OSI component driver occurs in 13 of 24 cases (52.2%) (`stress_attribution.md`; `case_studies.md`).

Partial agreement indicates that model attributions and component-level OSI decomposition align in slightly more than half of audited days—for example, high-stress validation cases on 2024-01-19 and 2024-01-06 where G8 matches a \(c_3\) limitation driver—but diverge in the remainder, including several low-OSI and shedding strata where G8 is the top gradient-attribution coalition while the component driver is \(c_2\) reserve margin. The benchmark demonstrates association between limitation-stack attributions and OSI outputs but does not fully validate gradient-attribution rankings against component physics. Stress explanations should be presented as consistent in a majority of case studies while flagging the 47.8% disagreement rate.

### 8.6.6 Cross-method validation and limitations

Permutation importance partially corroborates stress gradient-attribution rankings (Spearman ρ = 0.366) but disagrees with Dhaka demand gradient-attribution order (ρ = −0.564) (Table 7; `feature_importance.md`). On demand, permutation ranks regional supply block (G2) highest by mean MAE degradation (1.96 MW) while GradientSHAP ranks calendar/trend (G6) highest (|φ| = 162.34); engineered lags (G4) show high |φ| magnitude (101.26) but low permutation degradation (0.013 MW). On stress, limitation stack (G8) leads both gradient-attribution and permutation tables, though permutation deltas are numerically small (top stress permutation Δ ≈ 3.8×10⁻⁵).

Because no independent Captum-only attribution table is published separately from the integrated-gradient pipeline, gradient-attribution versus Captum agreement cannot be assessed directly; the relevant disagreement is between gradient-based attributions and permutation-based importance, especially on demand. Global GradientSHAP summaries are computed on validation windows (n = 20); case-study dual-path statistics cover 24 stratified days with only four on the test split. Attribution outputs describe model behaviour on the frozen sample and must not be interpreted as causal proof, complete OSI driver validation, or interchangeable rankings across methods. Practical use of S2 explanations should combine coalition gradient attributions, spatial attention (Figure 7), temporal weights (Figure 8), and component comparison (Figure 9) with explicit reporting of partial cross-method and dual-path agreement. Taken together, the explainability artefacts provide complementary transparency into model behaviour rather than independent validation of the underlying physical mechanisms.
