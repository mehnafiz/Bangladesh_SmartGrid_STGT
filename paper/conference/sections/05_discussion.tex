\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

The results show that one correlation-graph PF-STGT can produce coupled day-ahead load and stress forecasts from shared representations on Bangladesh national data. Below we discuss the main patterns and what they mean for this grid.

\subsection{Correlation Graphs Versus Geography}

Geographical adjacency is intuitive---divisions that share a border may exchange power---but it treats every border link equally regardless of how strongly loads actually co-move. On Bangladesh's national footprint, evening-peak demand correlations are positive across divisions, driven by shared growth, holidays, and seasonality rather than border proximity. At $\tau{=}0.65$, the correlation graph keeps 33 of 36 pairs; geographical adjacency keeps only 21 border links and misses divisions that move together without sharing a border.

A5 (geographical graph) slightly improves OSI MAE (0.0340) but raises demand error by 9.33~MW over S2, which shows that the best graph for stress is not necessarily the best for load. Geographical conditioning significantly worsens demand ($p{=}1.48{\times}10^{-4}$) because it forces message passing through a sparse, equal-weight skeleton. Correlation weighting instead gives graded edges that match predictive coupling and can be refined further by graph attention (Eq.~\eqref{eq:spatial}). Similar behaviour has been reported on other national-scale systems where synchronisation dominates local exchange \cite{Zhao2024GraphAttentionTransformer,Bentsen2023GraphTransformer}.

\subsection{Multi-Task Learning Trade-offs}

Demand and OSI draw on the same calendar, limitation, and generation context but answer different questions: regional megawatt levels versus a single bounded stress score. Shared encoding lets the model pick up limitation-related structure---also visible in attribution for G8 (limitation stack) and G6 (calendar/trend)---that a demand-only model would have to recover indirectly. Without the loss rescaling in Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}, demand gradients dominate and the OSI head barely trains.

Demand-only A4 reaches 86.89~MW, 1.76~MW below S2, because the stress head adds a mild regularising pull away from pure demand fitting. Joint training still delivers useful OSI output ($R^2{=}0.745$). In practice, a centre that needs only load forecasts may prefer A4; one that wants paired load and stress views can accept the small demand cost for much stronger stress predictions than B07 or classical baselines provide.

\subsection{Bangladesh Grid Context}

Three features of the dataset shape these findings. Nine divisions report shedding, reserve, and limitation in the same daily records, which makes composite stress supervision possible without extra telemetry. Dhaka dominates national demand ($\sim$35.7\%) and error, so macro metrics understate how sensitive planning is to the capital region; that concentration appears across all model families and looks more like a data-scale issue than a model failure. Finally, daily reporting with a seven-day lookback matches administrative practice, which helps explain why shallow recurrent and T-GCN models lag behind full transformer capacity on a densely connected correlation graph.

\subsection{Deployment Notes}

A single service could update regional evening-peak trajectories and a national OSI scalar from one seven-day input window each day. Operators might review both outputs during day-ahead planning, report Dhaka separately, and watch limitation-stack and calendar inputs flagged by attribution. We would expect shadow-mode deployment beside existing tools first, with division-level MAE checks and periodic stress-case review in place of formal prediction intervals. These are planning suggestions grounded in offline accuracy, not evidence of improved grid reliability or economic outcome in the field.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

All evidence comes from one Bangladesh timeline at daily resolution with internal benchmarks only; there is no external replication or live deployment trial. Statistical testing covers demand MAE; OSI gains lack hypothesis tests or intervals. Attribution reflects model behaviour, not causation, and demand attributions disagree with permutation rankings ($\rho{=}{-}0.564$). Uncertainty quantification and full S2 residual diagnostics were not completed.

Natural next steps include repeating the protocol on comparable South Asian systems, testing adaptive correlation graphs, adding probabilistic heads, and running operator-in-the-loop studies of dual-task explanation dashboards.
